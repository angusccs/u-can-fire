Glossary

A–B

Avalanche Method (Debt Repayment)

A strategy where you pay off debts starting with the highest interest rate first, regardless of balance. This saves the most money in interest.

Asset Allocation

How you divide your investments between things like stocks, bonds, or cash. It affects your risk and returns.

Buy and Hold

Long-term investing where you regularly buy investments and keep them for many years, ignoring short-term market ups and downs.

C–F

Coast FI

A stage where you’ve invested enough early that your money can grow on its own to reach your retirement goal — meaning you can “coast” and only need to cover your living expenses from work, not save aggressively.

CDIC (Canada Deposit Insurance Corporation)

Government insurance that protects money in Canadian bank accounts (up to certain limits) if a bank fails.

ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)

A low-cost, diversified investment that bundles lots of stocks or bonds into one product. ETFs are popular in Canada for simple, hands-off investing.

Emergency Fund

Cash savings set aside for unexpected expenses like job loss or car repairs. Your “starter” fund is $1,000–$2,000; your full fund is 3–6 months of expenses.

Financial Independence (FI)

Having enough savings and investments that you can choose how much you want to work, or not work at all.

G–I

HISA (High-Interest Savings Account)

A bank account that pays higher interest than a regular savings account. Good for emergency funds or short-term savings.

Index Fund

A type of investment that tracks a whole market (like the S&P 500 or a Canadian index). Index funds are simple, low-fee, and widely recommended.

Investment Risk

The chance that the value of your investments goes up or down. Higher-risk investments often grow more over time but fluctuate more.

J–M

Long-Term Investing

Buying investments and holding them for many years (often decades), instead of trying to time the market or trade frequently.

Margin Account

A type of account that lets you borrow money to invest. Not recommended for beginners.

P–S

Payday Loan

A very short-term loan with extremely high interest rates. These are considered dangerous because debt grows fast.

RRSP (Registered Retirement Savings Plan)

A tax-deferred account where contributions reduce your taxable income now, and you pay tax later when you withdraw in retirement.

Savings Rate

The percentage of your income you save or invest. Higher savings rates lead to faster financial independence.

Snowball Method (Debt Repayment)

A strategy where you pay off debts starting with the smallest balance first to build momentum and motivation — even if another debt has a higher interest rate.

T–Z

TFSA (Tax-Free Savings Account)

A Canadian account where your investments grow tax-free and withdrawals are tax-free. Useful for all income levels.

Volatility

How much an investment moves up or down in price. High volatility = bigger swings.
